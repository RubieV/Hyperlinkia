Careers

With the explosive increase of data in Internet era, the data-savvy professionals are more and more desired in various kinds of fields such as scientific research, medicine, businesses and government.

The supply of professionals who can work effectively with data is limited, and is reflected by rapidly rising salaries for data scientists, statisticians, and data analysts. It seems that the only problem with this career is supply and demand.

McKinsey and Company reports that by 2018, there will be 140,000-190,000 data related job postings that go unfulfilled. The authors also project a need for 1.5 million more managers and analysts with deep analytical and technical skills who can ask the right questions and consume the results of analysis of big data effectively.

In other words, the next three years is a veritable gold mine for data scientists. Be ready for the sexiest job of 21st century! But also take a look at who preceded you.

Scientific Research

Data scientists investigate complex problems through expertise in disciplines within the fields of mathematics, statistics, and computer science.

But the usage of data is not just limited to these fields. Actually, the essence of science is to systematically build and organize knowledge using data. This is done with scientific research. It is necessary to know how to collect, mine, analyse and interpret data nearly in every domain of science.

These are economics, biological sciences, health care, social sciences and humanities etc. Data scientists are of course master of the data process.

In the elective Research Methodology all these fields are touched upon, creating a broader level of appliance. With this broadened scientific, data scientist can certainly make a career in research.

Medicine

Medical facilities generate and process huge volumes of medical information and data. They have been increasing big data utilization. Big data is changing traditional medical research. The data gathered with tests in labs needs to be stored, processed, interpreted and made available as fast as possible.

You can imagine that time is of the essence: the longer it takes for the data to find the way to the correct physician or researcher, the more the patient suffers.

The use of giant online data databases, like the ones used for the Dutch Electronisch Patienten Dossier, makes this speedy process possible.

Apart from medical environments, the use of home medical devices is also increasing. These data are maybe not the most reliable, but can also help physicians determine the cause of suffering.

The question then is, how does this data find its way to the doctor? As you may have guessed, the use of databases can prove helpful in this case too. Both the data from medical facilities and from home have to be managed. That is where data science comes in.

The integration of data in the medical sphere is taught in the Bio-informatics elective. An career in medicine can prove quite interesting.

Business

Data enables companies to gain new insights into areas ranging from customer behaviour to how their businesses function. These data-driven findings can cause changes in for example marketing strategies and pricing.

Often products only see the light of day if they make it through rigorous statistical scrutiny. Since firms’ core product development is operating in a way that's governed by data, people in finance and business planning can't perform their function without data science.

Data scientists are very popular in the business sector. The rarity of data scientists already has some executives describing them as unicorns. Making career in business as a data scientist can be a wise and lucrative choice.

To work in business, data scientists do need some more field specific knowledge. The Business elective in the Data Science bachelor is designed to teach this knowledge.

Government

Governments provide a large variety of services, which both produce and require massive amounts of data, often unstructured and increasing in real-time. This data comes from numerous sources including historical, video, cell phones, geospatial, imagery, social media, and much more.

From crime prevention to transportation, health care, national security, revenue management, environmental stewardship and social services, governments wrestle every day with managing and using this data. There are already good examples of government using big data effectively.

The Troubled Families Initiative, for instance, has successfully combined data on families with a collaborative effort across public services at a local level.

Another example is the personal recruitment of DJ Patil by President Obama. He was just appointed to the newly created position, where the ex-LinkedIn chief scientist will work in a supervisory role for a broad analytics effort across the federal government. His duties will include shaping policies and practices across agencies, building data science partnerships with the private sector, and helping agencies recruit talent.

It shows that careers in government aren’t necessarily boring.
